**Detection Signals**

# What are the two signals, what does each one measure, what does each signal's output look like, and how are they combined into a single confidence score?

Signal A is burstiness. It looks at how varied the sentence lengths are. Human writers naturally mix short and long sentences. AI writing tends to keep sentences at a similar length because it is optimizing for smooth output, not making a deliberate choice about rhythm. The analyzer measures the variation in sentence lengths and turns that into a score. Very uniform lengths get a score near 1.0. Very uneven lengths get a score near 0.0. The output is a single number between 0.0 and 1.0. No API call needed.

Signal B is stylometrics. It looks at three habits that show up differently in AI and human writing:

Transition words. AI text uses phrases like "Furthermore" and "Additionally" at a rate most human writers do not. Even one or two in a short piece is worth flagging.

Contractions. AI text almost never uses them. "I am" instead of "I'm", "will not" instead of "won't". Human casual writing is full of them.

Sentence capitalization. AI text starts every sentence with a capital. All-lowercase writing is a strong sign of a real person.

The three sub-scores get averaged into one stylometric score between 0.0 and 1.0.

To get the final confidence score: confidence = 0.5 x burstiness + 0.5 x stylometric. Equal weights because there is no evidence yet that one signal is more accurate than the other.

**Uncertainty Representation**

# What does a confidence score of 0.6 mean to the system? How are raw signal outputs mapped to a calibrated score? What thresholds separate the three label variants?

A 0.6 means the system leans toward AI but is not confident enough to say so. It falls in the uncertain band. The reader sees a label that says the system could not make a clear call, not a watered-down AI verdict.

The cutoffs are: 0.80 or above gets the AI label. 0.20 or below gets the human label. Everything in between gets the uncertain label.

The cutoffs are wide on purpose. A verdict only goes in the confident direction when the evidence is strong. A score of 0.60 leans one way but the system calls it uncertain rather than committing. That makes the confident verdicts more trustworthy when they do appear.

The scores are not probabilities. A 0.85 does not mean there is an 85% chance the text is AI generated. It means the writing habits measured by both signals are strongly consistent with AI text. The label says "likely" rather than quoting a percentage for that reason.

---

**Transparency Label**

# What exact text will each label variant show? Write all three out before building anything.

High confidence AI (score >= 0.80):
"This content was likely generated by AI. Our system analyzed writing patterns and detected strong indicators of AI authorship. This label is not a judgment. It is context for you as a reader."

Uncertain (0.20 < score < 0.80):
"Our system was unable to make a confident determination about the origin of this content. The signals were mixed or inconclusive. Treat this label as informational, not definitive."

High confidence human (score <= 0.20):
"This content appears to be human written. Our system found no strong indicators of AI generation in the writing patterns it analyzed."

**Appeals Workflow**

# Who can submit an appeal, what do they provide, what does the system do, and what would a human reviewer see?

Any creator with a content_id from a prior submission can appeal. They send the content_id and a written explanation of why they think the verdict is wrong.

The system looks up the original entry. If it does not exist it returns a 404. If an appeal already exists it returns a 409. One appeal per submission, no exceptions. If the entry is clean, the system attaches the appeal reason and timestamp and changes the status from classified to under_review.

A human reviewer looking at the queue sees the original signal scores, the confidence score, the verdict, the label that was shown to the user, and the creator's explanation. Everything needed to make a judgment call is right there.

Nothing gets reclassified automatically. If the system reclassified on every appeal, anyone could game it with a one-line boilerplate reason.

---

**Anticipated Edge Cases**

# What types of content will this system handle poorly? Name at least two specific scenarios.

Poetry is the most predictable problem. A villanelle or a song chorus uses deliberate repetition, fixed line lengths, and a small controlled vocabulary by design. The burstiness signal reads uniform line lengths as AI-like. The stylometric signal may read low vocabulary variety the same way. Both are actual features of the form, not signs of AI generation. The system cannot tell the difference between "uniform because a machine wrote it" and "uniform because the poem requires it."

Non-native English writing will get flagged more often than it should. A writer being careful with grammar produces more controlled sentences, fewer contractions, and more consistent capitalization than a native speaker writing casually. Both signals read that as AI-like. There is no way to fix this within the current setup. The label text acknowledges it by saying "this label is not a judgment."

Very short text is a softer problem. With only two sentences, the burstiness check barely has enough data to work with. The system will return a score, but that score reflects much less evidence than it would for a longer piece.

## Architecture

A request hits the rate limiter first, which blocks anything over 10 requests per minute from the same IP. Requests that pass go to the submission handler, which checks the body and sends the text through the detection pipeline where both analyzers run independently, the confidence scorer combines their scores, the label generator picks one of three label variants, and the audit logger writes the full record before the response goes back. The appeal flow is separate: it finds the existing log entry, attaches the appeal, and flips the status without touching the original classification.

## AI Tool Plan

**M3: Submission endpoint and first signal**

Spec sections to provide: Detection Signals (burstiness only) and the Architecture diagram.

What to ask for: a Flask app skeleton with a POST /submit route that takes text and creator_id, plus a standalone burstiness_score function that takes a string and returns a float. Ask for them separately so the function can be tested before being wired in.

How to verify: run burstiness_score on three inputs before touching the endpoint. A short uniform paragraph should score above 0.7. A passage with wildly varied sentence lengths should score below 0.3. A single sentence should not crash.

**M4: Second signal and confidence scoring**

Spec sections to provide: full Detection Signals section, Uncertainty Representation section, and the Architecture diagram.

What to ask for: a stylometric_score function with the same interface as burstiness_score, and a separate combine_scores function that takes both scores and returns the weighted result.

How to verify: run a clearly AI paragraph and a clearly human paragraph through the full pipeline. If the combined scores differ by less than 0.2, the signals are not doing meaningful work and need investigation.

**M5: Production layer**

Spec sections to provide: Transparency Label section, Appeals Workflow section, and the Architecture diagram.

What to ask for: a generate_label function that maps a confidence score to the correct label string, the POST /appeal endpoint, the GET /log endpoint, and rate limiting on POST /submit using Flask Limiter.

How to verify: pass scores of 0.9, 0.5, and 0.1 directly to generate_label and confirm all three strings match what is written in the spec. Then submit a classification, post an appeal using the content_id, and confirm GET /log shows the entry as under_review with the appeal reason attached. Confirm a 429 comes back after 10 rapid requests to POST /submit.
