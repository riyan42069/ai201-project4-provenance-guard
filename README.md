# Provenance Guard

A backend system that classifies whether a piece of text was written by a human or generated by AI. It returns a confidence score, a plain-English transparency label, and logs every decision. Creators can file an appeal if they disagree with a verdict.

---

## Detection Signals

The system runs two independent checks. Each returns a score from 0.0 to 1.0 where higher means more AI-like.

**Burstiness** measures sentence length variance. Human writing is rhythmically uneven. AI writing is metronomically consistent because the model optimizes for fluency rather than making deliberate rhetorical choices. Low variance scores near 1.0. High variance scores near 0.0.

**Stylometrics** measures three surface habits: how often formal transition words appear ("Furthermore", "Additionally"), whether contractions are present (AI almost never uses them), and whether sentences are properly capitalized (casual human writing is often all lowercase). The three sub-scores are averaged.

These two were chosen because they measure different things. Burstiness catches rhythmic uniformity. Stylometrics catches vocabulary and grammar formality. A text would have to fool both independently to get a wrong verdict.

For a real deployment I would add passive voice density as a fourth feature and retrain the weights on a labeled sample from the actual platform. I originally planned to use type-token ratio as one of the stylometric features but dropped it during testing because at typical submission lengths (50 to 300 words) both human and AI text consistently scored between 0.85 and 0.92, making the feature useless as a discriminator.

## Confidence Scoring

```
confidence = 0.5 x burstiness + 0.5 x stylometric
```

The score is not a probability. A 0.85 does not mean there is an 85% chance the text is AI generated. It means the writing patterns are strongly consistent with what AI text looks like. The label text uses "likely" rather than quoting a percentage for that reason.

Verdicts are assigned at wide thresholds on purpose. A score has to reach 0.80 before the system calls something AI generated, and 0.20 before it calls something human written. Anything in between is uncertain. Narrower thresholds would produce more confident-sounding labels on weaker evidence.

**High-confidence AI example (score 0.904):**

Text: a paragraph packed with transition words, no contractions, uniform 10 to 12 word sentences throughout.

```json
{
  "content_id": "feafc481-0589-4997-9edd-611dc75a1d15",
  "attribution": "ai",
  "confidence": 0.904,
  "signals": { "burstiness": 0.808, "stylometric": 1.0 }
}
```

Both signals agreed strongly. The stylometric score hit 1.0 because the text used six transition words, no contractions, and capitalized every sentence.

**Lower-confidence uncertain example (score 0.295):**

Text: casual all-lowercase writing, erratic sentence lengths, one contraction.

```json
{
  "content_id": "1de1eeb3-bbe2-44af-939d-b139ac0ebba1",
  "attribution": "uncertain",
  "confidence": 0.2953,
  "signals": { "burstiness": 0.3167, "stylometric": 0.2738 }
}
```

Both signals went in the human direction. The score of 0.295 sits just above the 0.20 threshold so the verdict is uncertain rather than human. The system leans human but is not confident enough to commit.

The gap between these two examples is 0.609. That is the signal doing real work.

## Transparency Label

Three variants based on the confidence score. The exact text shown to readers:

**High confidence AI (score >= 0.80):**

> "This content was likely generated by AI. Our system analyzed writing patterns and detected strong indicators of AI authorship. This label is not a judgment. It is context for you as a reader."

**Uncertain (0.20 < score < 0.80):**

> "Our system was unable to make a confident determination about the origin of this content. The signals were mixed or inconclusive. Treat this label as informational, not definitive."

**High confidence human (score <= 0.20):**

> "This content appears to be human written. Our system found no strong indicators of AI generation in the writing patterns it analyzed."

## Known Limitations

**Poetry will get mislabeled.** A villanelle, a haiku, or a song chorus is written under strict formal constraints: controlled repetition, fixed line lengths, deliberate syllabic uniformity. The burstiness signal will read uniform line lengths as AI-like. The stylometric signal will read low vocabulary variety as AI-like. Both of those are real features of lyric poetry. The system has no concept of literary form and cannot tell the difference between "uniform because a machine wrote it" and "uniform because that is what the form requires."

**Non-native English writing scores higher for AI likelihood.** A writer being careful with grammar produces more controlled sentence structure, fewer informal markers, and more consistent capitalization than a native speaker writing casually. Both signals read this as AI-like. This is not fixable by adjusting the thresholds. It is a direct consequence of what the signals measure. The label text says "this label is not a judgment" specifically because of this.

## Spec Reflection

**One way the spec helped:** Writing the edge cases section before building anything saved a real debugging session during Milestone 4. When the formal academic writing sample scored 0.65 and came back as uncertain, the instinct was to check whether something was broken. The edge cases section already said formal human writing would score high because both signals treat academic formality the same as AI patterns. That turned thirty minutes of potential confusion into a one-sentence confirmation the system was working correctly.

**One way the implementation diverged:** Type-token ratio was listed in the spec as one of the three stylometric sub-features. It was dropped and replaced with sentence capitalization ratio. When both the clearly AI sample and the clearly human sample returned TTR values of 0.88 and 0.87 respectively, the feature was adding noise rather than signal. Sentence capitalization turned out to be stable at short text lengths and clearly different between casual human writing and AI writing. The spec was a hypothesis. Testing showed that hypothesis was wrong for this specific use case.
